What Is Generative Engine Optimization (GEO)? A Med Spa Owner's Guide

Quick answer

Generative engine optimization (GEO) is the practice of structuring your website content so AI tools like ChatGPT, Perplexity, and Google AI Overviews cite your business when someone asks a question. Unlike SEO, which competes for ranked links, GEO competes for the answer itself. For med spas, that means showing up when someone types 'best Botox clinic near me' into an AI chatbot.

The Short Answer First

Generative engine optimization (GEO) is how you get your med spa cited inside AI-generated answers instead of just ranked in a list of links.

When someone opens ChatGPT and types "best place for lip fillers near me," they're not getting ten blue links. They're getting a paragraph that names specific providers. GEO is how you become one of those names.

That's the whole concept. Everything else is execution.

Why This Is Different From What You've Done Before

Traditional SEO is a ranking game. You optimize pages, build links, and climb Google's results until you're on page one. Users see your link, click it, and (hopefully) book.

GEO is a citation game. AI engines — Google AI Overviews, ChatGPT, Perplexity — read thousands of sources, synthesize them into a direct answer, and pick a handful of businesses to mention. If your content is structured the way these models prefer, you get mentioned. If it's written like a brochure, you don't.

The stakes are real. A 2025 study found that AI Overviews appear for roughly 47% of health and beauty searches. That's nearly half your potential patients seeing an AI-generated answer before a single link loads.

What AI Engines Actually Want

AI models aren't impressed by keyword density or meta tags the way old-school SEO tools were. They're looking for:

  • Clear, direct answers to specific questions ("How long does Botox last?" gets a better response from a page that answers it in two sentences than one that buries the answer in a 1,200-word essay)
  • Structured data — FAQ schema, local business schema, review schema — that tells the model what your content is about
  • Consistent business information — name, address, phone, and hours that match across Google, Yelp, RealSelf, Healthgrades, and your own site
  • Credible citations — local press mentions, partnerships with dermatologists, links from medical directories
  • Genuine reviews — not just star ratings, but text reviews that mention specific treatments and outcomes

A Concrete Med Spa Example

Say your Botox page currently reads: "At [Spa Name], we offer world-class Botox treatments delivered by our experienced team in a luxurious environment."

An AI engine reads that and learns almost nothing useful. It can't cite you for "how much does Botox cost near me" or "how many units of Botox for forehead lines" because you haven't answered those questions.

Now imagine you restructure that page with a section that reads:

"Botox at our clinic typically ranges from $12 to $15 per unit. A forehead treatment uses 10 to 30 units depending on your anatomy, so most patients spend $150 to $450. Results last 3 to 4 months. We recommend a follow-up at week two to assess results."

That's a citable answer. When someone asks ChatGPT "how much does Botox cost near me," your page is a candidate for the response. Add FAQ schema markup around that content and you've made it even easier for AI models to extract and attribute it.

This is GEO in practice — not a technical trick, but a content strategy shift.

How GEO Fits Into Your Existing Marketing

You don't have to throw out your SEO work. A lot of it still matters because AI engines use traditional ranking signals to gauge credibility. A page that ranks well on Google is more likely to be pulled into an AI Overview than one buried on page four.

What GEO adds on top:

Content restructuring. Convert service pages from marketing copy to resource pages. Answer the real questions patients ask — "does laser hair removal hurt?", "what's the difference between Sculptra and Juvederm?", "how many sessions does CoolSculpting take?"

Schema markup. Technical tags that tell AI engines your content format, your business details, and your FAQs. Read our full med spa SEO guide for how schema fits into your broader strategy.

Authority signals. AI engines weight citations from recognized sources. A mention in a local publication, a profile on RealSelf, or a quote from your lead injector in a health article all help.

Review content. Encourage patients to write reviews that mention specific treatments. "Amazing Botox results" is less useful than "I came in for 20 units of Botox on my forehead and the results were natural and lasted four months." The specifics get picked up.

The Local Angle: Every Market Is Competitive

Every city has dozens of med spas competing for the same patients. When someone asks Perplexity for laser treatment recommendations in your area, a handful of names will come up. Those names won't be randomly selected — they'll be the practices with the most structured, credible, question-answering content online.

That's actually good news if you move early. GEO is still new enough that most med spas haven't done it intentionally. The practices that structure their content now, build their schema, and earn consistent local citations will own those AI responses for years.

Where to Start

If you're running a med spa and want to show up in AI answers, here's a practical first pass:

  1. Audit your top 5 service pages. Read them like a patient would. Do they answer the questions patients actually ask? If not, add a plain-language Q&A section to each.
  2. Add FAQ schema to those pages. This is a half-day technical task, but the payoff is outsized.
  3. Check your NAP consistency. Search your business name and make sure the address, phone, and hours are identical everywhere they appear online.
  4. Ask for specific reviews. Coach happy patients to mention the treatment, the result, and the timeframe.
  5. Get one or two local citations. Reach out to a local health blogger, submit a profile to a medical directory, or see if your chamber of commerce has a member spotlight.

None of this requires a complete website overhaul. Small, targeted changes to content and structure compound quickly in AI systems.

Frequently asked questions

What is the difference between SEO and GEO?+–

SEO gets your website ranked in traditional search results so users click a link. GEO gets your content cited inside AI-generated answers, so your med spa is the answer users see before they ever click anything. Both matter right now, but AI-generated answers are eating an increasing share of search traffic.

Does GEO replace SEO for med spas?+–

No, GEO and SEO work together. Strong SEO signals like backlinks, page speed, and technical structure still feed AI engines the credibility data they need. Think of GEO as a layer on top of SEO that makes your content readable and trustworthy to AI models, not a replacement for the foundation.

How long does it take to see results from GEO?+–

Most med spas see AI citations within 6 to 12 weeks after implementing structured content, FAQ schema, and consistent NAP data across directories. The timeline depends on how much existing content you have and how competitive your local market is. In a competitive market, quality matters more than speed.

Which AI tools should a med spa care about?+–

Right now the three that drive the most discovery traffic are Google AI Overviews, ChatGPT (with browsing enabled), and Perplexity. Each pulls from different data sources, but they all reward the same things: clear answers, structured content, authoritative citations, and consistent business information across the web.

What content changes make the biggest GEO impact for med spas?+–

Adding direct, question-format answers to your service pages, implementing FAQ schema markup, earning mentions from credible local sources, and keeping your Google Business Profile current. Service pages that answer 'what is,' 'how much does,' and 'how long does' questions in plain language get cited far more often than vague marketing copy.
